Inspired by a myth of buying heaven and hell

In the Middle Ages, the act of selling indulgences, which served as a remission of punishment in Christianity, got out of hand. To finance crusades and cathedrals, church pardoners would go around selling indulgences for large sums of money. They went as far as promising the buyers eternal salvation. Commoners, believing they were buying "a piece of heaven" for themselves and their loved ones, paid a lot for those indulgences.

The myth begins with Martin Luther, a seminal figure in the Protestant Reformation, being provoked by these acts. As a counteract, Luther went to a priest and asked to buy hell. The surprised priest, in his greed, was glad to sell Luther a document stating him as the sole owner of hell.

Luther then went to the town square, showing this document to the people. Shouting that they should stop buying indulgences, since he was the sole owner of Hell, and won't let anyone enter it. This rendered the plot of buying heaven unnecessary and caused the church trouble.

Another version of this myth states that a deviant merchant was the one to buy hell, which he sold back to the church for some great returns.
